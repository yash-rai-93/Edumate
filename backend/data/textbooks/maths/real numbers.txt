CLASS 10 MATHS – CHAPTER: REAL NUMBERS (RATIONAL & IRRATIONAL)  
EXTENSIVE NOTES

---------------------------------------------------
1. Rational Numbers
---------------------------------------------------
A rational number is any number that can be written in the form p/q, where:
- p and q are integers
- q ≠ 0

Examples:
1/2, -3/5, 7, 0, 4.25 (= 17/4)

Properties:
- Every integer is a rational number.
- Rational numbers can be terminating or recurring decimals.

Terminating decimal: 0.25, 1.75  
Recurring decimal: 0.333..., 2.717171...

---------------------------------------------------
2. Irrational Numbers
---------------------------------------------------
A number that cannot be written in the form p/q.  
Its decimal expansion is non-terminating and non-repeating.

Examples:
√2, √5, π, 0.1010010001...

---------------------------------------------------
3. Real Numbers
---------------------------------------------------
All rational and irrational numbers together form the set of real numbers.

Real numbers can be represented on the number line.

---------------------------------------------------
4. Decimal Expansions
---------------------------------------------------
A rational number p/q has:
A. Terminating decimal expansion iff  
   the prime factors of q are ONLY 2 or 5.  
   Example: 7/8 → 0.875 (denominator: 2³)

B. Non-terminating recurring decimal expansion iff  
   q has primes other than 2 or 5.  
   Example: 5/11 → 0.454545...

---------------------------------------------------
5. Euclid’s Division Lemma
---------------------------------------------------
Given two positive integers a and b, there exist unique integers q and r such that:
a = bq + r  
where 0 ≤ r < b

Used to find:
- HCF (GCD) of two numbers  
- To prove irrationality

---------------------------------------------------
6. Euclid’s Division Algorithm (for HCF)
---------------------------------------------------
Steps:
1. Apply a = bq + r  
2. Replace a with b, b with r  
3. Repeat until r = 0  
4. Last non-zero remainder = HCF

Example: HCF(225, 135)
225 = 135×1 + 90  
135 = 90×1 + 45  
90 = 45×2 + 0  
HCF = 45

---------------------------------------------------
7. Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic
---------------------------------------------------
Every composite number can be expressed uniquely as a product of prime numbers (apart from order).

Example:
140 = 2² × 5 × 7  
980 = 2² × 5 × 7²

Applications:
- Finding HCF  
- Finding LCM  
- Proving irrationality  
- Terminating/non-terminating decimals

---------------------------------------------------
8. Proving Irrationality
---------------------------------------------------
Most common proofs:
1. √2 is irrational  
2. √3 is irrational  
3. √5 is irrational  

General method:
- Assume √n = p/q  
- Square both sides  
- Reach contradiction  
- Conclude √n is irrational

---------------------------------------------------
9. Relationship Between HCF and LCM
---------------------------------------------------
For any two positive integers a and b:
HCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b

Example:
a = 12, b = 18  
HCF = 6, LCM = 36  
6 × 36 = 12 × 18 = 216

---------------------------------------------------
10. Important Examples
---------------------------------------------------
1. Show that 1.1010010001… is irrational.  
   → Non-terminating, non-repeating decimal.

2. Write 2.75 in p/q form.  
   → 2.75 = 275/100 = 11/4

3. For 17/8, check decimal expansion.  
   → 8 = 2³ → terminating.

4. For 21/14, simplify first.  
   → = 3/2 → terminating.

---------------------------------------------------
11. Key Points to Remember
---------------------------------------------------
- Rational → terminating or recurring decimals  
- Irrational → non-terminating, non-recurring  
- Euclid’s lemma used for HCF  
- Prime factorization used for LCM and terminating decimal test  
- √(non-perfect square) = irrational  

---------------------------------------------------
